Iron has been fused along its length, forming a narrow shaft, while the knuckle has been capped in dark brass and notched into a crude but functional ward. The nail bed remains visible beneath the metal, preserved rather than removed.

This key was not made in secrecy. It was made with witnesses.

When the Hollowed Saints sealed the Crypt, they believed no living hand should ever open it again. Authority, they reasoned, must die with them. So they took the thumb of a presiding priest—some say a volunteer, others say the last to object—and fashioned it into the lock’s answer.

The choice of bone was deliberate. In the rites of the church, the thumb marked vows, sealed confessions, rang bells, and traced absolution. It was the digit of office, not strength. By turning it into a key, the Saints ensured that only what remained of their authority could grant passage.

The iron binding prevents decay. The bone does not rot. It does not forgive.

Those who hold the key sometimes feel a pressure against their own thumb, as though something is testing the shape of their grip, measuring whether they still deserve to open what lies beyond.

> _“When the hand fails, the office remains. When the office fails, only the bone remembers.”_
