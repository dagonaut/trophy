
A small handbell of dull bronze, its surface worn smooth where Father Lorian’s fingers once rested. When rung, the sound does not travel far, yet it is impossible to ignore. Those who hear it feel called to it, even if they do not know why.

Lorian rang it to summon the grieving, the lost, the uncertain. He believed that if people stood close enough, sorrow would lessen by proximity alone. For a time, it did.

After his death, the bell remained. Others rang it without his restraint or patience. The call sharpened. What once invited began to _command_. The sound learned how to compel instead of invite.

Now the bell does no longer gathers bodies. It just gathers grief.

_“He rang it so no one would be alone. But after he was gone, they had no where to go.”_

The Better Stuff:

## **Calling Bell of Father Lorian**

A simple hand bell of dulled bronze, small enough to be carried but heavier than it appears. When rung the sound does not echo, but _gathers_.

Lorian used it to summon pilgrims to the church when it was still a place of passage rather than containment. It was not a call to confession, but an invitation: to come forward, to be witnessed, to set down what could not be carried alone.

The Choir assumed the bell’s function in voice alone. The summons remained, but the release was forgotten.

The bell has never been cracked, bent, or reforged. It does not carry grief itself. It only gathers those who answer.

> _“The bell gathers those ready to be heard. He who listens can let them rest.”_

---

### Subtle Truth (for you, not the players)

- The Calling Bell **brings spirits, echoes, and grief-bound presences into attention**.
    
- It does not soothe, banish, or control them.
    
- It creates readiness, not resolution.
    

Only Father Lorian’s Echo can complete what the bell begins.

---

### How the Pairing Reads in Play (Without Spelling It Out)

- The bell calls.
    
- The reliquary holds.
    
- Neither resolves anything alone.
    

In the Cistern:

- Ringing the bell draws the sorrow-bound spirits to the surface.
    
- Opening the Reliquary releases Lorian’s Echo.
    
- The Echo answers the call — not by commanding the spirits, but by **letting them go**.
    

From the outside, this looks like:

- Sound, then stillness.
    
- Gathering, then dispersal.
    
- The water remembering how to be empty.
    

---

### Why This Is Not Obvious (and Why That’s Good)

- The bell looks like a summoning tool, not a release mechanism.
    
- The reliquary looks like a dangerous artifact, not a mercy.
    
- The logic only becomes clear when players understand Lorian’s philosophy:
    
    - grief must pass through
        
    - calling and releasing are separate acts
        
    - containment without closure poisons the system
        

Players who miss this may still succeed — but at greater cost.

Players who understand it will feel like they **restored a broken idea**, not solved a puzzle.
